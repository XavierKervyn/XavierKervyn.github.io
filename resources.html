Resources

A compilation of lecture notes, moocs and webpages which I have found being worth browsing along the way. Unless stated otherwise, all resources are available in English. This page is under construction, so it might be a bit messy in some places :) Some links might also be broken; in that case please do reach out and I'll try to fix it.

Undergraduate

Mathematics

  1. Hundreds of exercises (in French) to practice differential calculus and analysis: Analyse - recueil d’exercices et aide mémoire. (Jacques Douchet) Vol.1 and Vol.2. I used both of these extensively in my first year at EPFL.
  2. The associated textbook for more details (in French): Calcul différentiel et intégral. (Jacques Douchet).
  3. Check out the videos of 3Blue1Brown, Mathemaniac, Mathologer, Michael Penn, Mu Prime Maths, Numberphile, and blackpenredpen on YouTube (integration, differential equations, etc.).

Physics

Statistical Physics

The following is based on a Master-level course I took while at ETHZ (3rd year of my BSc. in Physics), having already had an education in Thermodynamics.
  1. I found the first chapters quite clear: Statistical Physics (2 ed), Huang
  2. These were the lecture notes of the course I took, lectured by Prof. Manfred Sigrist.
  3. As always, I can’t but recommend checking out Prof. Tong’s lecture notes (it might be worth checking out the notes on Statistical Field theory as well, but this is definitely graduate level).

Classical Electrodynamics

  1. The perfect book for an introduction at an undergraduate level: Introduction to electrodynamics (4. ed), Griffiths
  2. Very famous, but quite old, and the problems are notoriously very difficult: Classical Electrodynamics, Jackson
  3. Collaborative solutions to the Problems in the Jackson: Physics is Beautiful
  4. My favourite, very complete and with lots of exercises: Modern Electrodynamics, Zangwill

Quantum Mechanics

  1. On the path integral approach to Quantum Mechanics, by the great Feynman: Quantum Mechanics and Path Integrals, Feynman. For a first contact with QM, I would rather recommend the Hamiltonian approach (eg. Griffiths).
  2. Lecture of Barton Zwiebach at MIT in 2018: Quantum Physics III
  3. Extract of the course on QM at the University of Cambridge: Time dependent Perturbation Theory
  4. Perfect for a first contact with Quantum Mechanics at an undergraduate level: Introduction to Quantum Mechanics, Griffiths
  5. Notes of Noah Miller (Harvard) on the use of representation theory in QM, very well written (PDF): Representation Theory and Quantum Mechanics
  6. Very interesting book on the use of symmetries in QM by Zwicky (PDF): Symmetries of Quantum Mechanics
  7. Good book on the use of group theory in QM: Quantum Theory, Groups and Representations, Woit
  8. Representation theory in QM (PDF): Understanding Quantum Mechanical Systems with Spherical Symmetry via Representations of Lie Groups, Greif
  9. Lecture on Quantum Groups at Stanford
  10. Woit's lecture on Quantum Groups in 2009
  11. Dozens of problems in Quantum Mechanics, with solutions: Problems and Solutions in Quantum Mechanics, Tamvakis

Solid State Physics

  1. A reference, well-written: Introduction to Solid state Physics, Kittel
  2. Probably the one that is the most widely used: Solid-State-Physics, Ashcroft & Mermin
  3. Collaborative solutions to Ashcroft and Mermin: dileepma
  4. Lots of exercises and course summary: Understanding solid state physics - problems and solutions, Cazaux
  5. Another batch of exercises to prepare for the exam: Solid State Physics: Problems and Solutions, Laszlo & Martin
  6. Lots of exercises, some of them in solid state physics: Problems and Solutions on Solid State Physics, Relativity and Miscellaneous Topics, Yung-Kuo

Particle and Nuclear Physics

  1. Good introductory book on the subject: The Basis of Nuclear and Particle Physics, by Belyaev and Ross.
  2. Summary of the experimental concepts: Techniques for Nuclear and Particle Physics Experiments, W.R. Leo.
  3. Another good textbook on the subject: Particle Physics (3rd. ed.), by B.R. Martin.
  4. To practice before the exam: Problems and Solutions of Atomic, Nuclear and Particle Physics, by Yung-Kuo Lim.

Graduate

The following are from the courses I took or am currently taking at the University of Cambridge. I have also included some that I've browsed for my independent study. As you might notice, my interests are heavily HEP & GR-focused!

Theoretical Physics

Quantum Field Theory

  1. Peskin & Schroeder, An Introduction to Quantum Field Theory. The reference textbook, but very long.
  2. Nair, Quantum Field Theory, A Modern Perspective. Includes strongly coupled theories.
  3. Tom Lancaster, QFT for the Gifted amateur, for a general scientific audience.
  4. Tong, Lecture notes on QFT, used for the first part of the course in Cambridge.
  5. Tong, Gauge Theory. Additional, but useful details.
  6. Wingate, Advanced Quantum Field Theory. Used for the second part of the course in Cambridge.
  7. Skinner, Advanced Quantum Field Theory. Different version of the course in Cambridge, more advanced.

General Relativity

  1. Caroll, Spacetime and Geometry. An excellent introduction to GR.
  2. Aretakis, Lecture Notes on General Relativity, Columbia University.
  3. Harvey Reall, Part III General Relativity. Lecture notes used at Cambridge.
  4. Wald, General Relativity. The reference textbook.

Symmetries in Physics, Lie Groups & Representation Theory

  1. Hall, Lie Groups, Lie Algebras and Representations, the reference textbook.
  2. Humphreys, Introduction to Lie Algebras and Representation Theory slightly easier to read.
  3. Ashok Das, Lie Groups and Lie Algebras for Physicists, lacking some maths but fairly intuitive
  4. Ian Lim, Symmetries, Field and Particles, based on the course of Cambridge (Dorey)
  5. Fuchs, Schweigert, Symmetries, Lie Algebras and Representations: a Graduate course for Physicists. Very good.

Supersymmetry

  1. The lecture notes of Prof. Tong, on SUSY & Duality. Prof. Tong was my lecturer in Cambridge.
  2. Muller-Kirsten, Introduction to Supersymmetry. I used this book a few times to check calculations, most of them are done in great detail, where other authors would just omit the steps.
  3. It can also be worth lookg at Prof. Tong's notes on Gauge Theory, especially when looking at (S)QCD.

String Theory

  1. Tong, String Theory. Notes I used in Cambridge.
  2. Polchinsky, String Theory, Vol.1 & Vol.2. The reference textbook.
  3. John D. Qualls, Lectures on Conformal Field theory
  4. Douglas, Gauntlett and Gross, Strings and Geometry. Collection of papers on topological string theory.
  5. Vafa et Al., Miror Symmetry, Clay Mathematics Monographs. Reference textbook on topological string theory.
  6. The amazing String Theory Wiki with reviews, resources, conferences, etc.
  7. Kevin Costello, Topological holography on PIRSA
  8. Hirosi Ooguri's lectures on Topological String theory (YouTube).
  9. Michael Penn's videos on the Witt algebra and the Virasoro algebra (YouTube).
  10. A few of Costello's super advanced talks: String theory for Mathematicians, and Topological strings, twistors, and Skyrmions.

Solitons, Instantons, Twistor Theory

  1. Manton, Topological Solitons
  2. Dunajski, Solitons, Instantons and Twistors
  3. Adamo, Lectures on Twistor Theory

Gauge/gravity duality, AdS/CFT

  1. Zaffaroni, Introduction to the AdS/CFT correspondence
  2. Veronika E. Hubeny, The AdS/CFT correspondence
  3. Mark van Raamsdonk, Lectures on Gravity and Entanglement
  4. TASI Lectures on the Emergence of Bulk Physics in AdS/CFT
  5. Johanna Erdmenger, Introduction to Gauge/Gravity Duality
  6. Gary Horowitz, Joseph Polchinksy, Gauge/Gravity duality.
  7. Johanna Erdmenger, Martin Ammon, Gauge/Gravity duality, Foundations and Applications
  8. Ian Lim, Gauge/Gravity duality (Aron Wall)
  9. Great talk from Juan Maldacena on YouTube, Wormholes and quantum entanglement.
  10. Kevin Costello once again: A Mathematician Looks a Supergravity and AdS/CFT.
  11. Joao Penedones' overview of boostrap and AdS amplitudes (YouTube).

Celestial holography

  1. Strominger, Lectures on the Infrared Structure of Gravity and Gauge Theories
  2. Fiorucci, Leaky covariant phase spaces
  3. Raclariu, Lectures on Celestial holography
  4. Very advanced talk by Kevin Costello on Celestial holography and Twistors (I don't think I understood most of it though).
  5. Great talk by Sabrina Pasterski on Building Celestial Holography, which is nicely complemented by Andrea Puhm's review of the Recent advances in Celestial Holography.

Integrability

  1. Niklas Beisert, Introduction to integrability.
  2. Misha Feigin, Introduction to Integrability Lecture Series.
  3. Till Bargheer, Introduction to Integrability.
  4. SISSA, Integrable Quantum Field Theory.
  5. Exhaustive (and long!) Review of AdS/CFT Integrability by Beisert and the likes.
  6. Check out the Gentle introduction to AdS3/CFT2 integrability by Prof. Alessandro Sfondrini on YouTube.
