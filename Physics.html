Ressources in Physics

Unless stated otherwise, all ressources are available in English.

General websites

  1. Archives of the Institute for Theoretical Physics at ETH Zürich: ITP Lecture Archive. Lots of lecture notes, sometimes along with the exercise sheets.
  2. Archives of the lectures at MIT: MIT OpenCourseware. Quite time-consuming but always good to get a different explanation on a given topic.
  3. Teaching page of Prof. Tong at the University of Cambridge: David Tong. Prof. Tong has given quite a few lectures at the University of Cambridge. I really appreciate his pedagogy, can't but recommend having a look at his lecture notes!
  4. Ressources of the Archimedeans, the Cambridge University Mathematical Society.
  5. Archives (lectures notes + example sheets) of the Part III in Applied Mathematics at the University of Cambridge
  6. Archives of the Examples (exercise sheets) for the Mathematical Tripos of the University of Cambridge.
  7. Archives (lectures notes + example sheets) of the Department of Pure Mathematics and Mathematical Statistics at the University of Cambridge
  8. Online courses at the Perimeter Institute
  9. Excellent lectures on the Geometrical anatomy of Theoretical Physics by Prof. Schüller
  10. Lecture courses of Prof. Mark Thomson, University of Cambridge

Classical Electrodynamics

  1. The perfect book for an introduction to ED at an undergraduate level: Introduction to electrodynamics (4. ed), Griffiths
  2. Very famous, but quite old, and the problems are notoriously very difficult: Classical Electrodynamics, Jackson
  3. Collaborative solutions to the Problems in the Jackson: Physics is Beautiful
  4. My favourite, very complete and with lots of exercises: Modern Electrodynamics, Zangwill

Quantum Mechanics

  1. On the path integral approach to Quantum Mechanics, by the great Feynman: Quantum Mechanics and Path Integrals, Feynman. For a first contact with QM, I would rather recommend the Hamiltonian approach (eg. Griffiths).
  2. Lecture of Barton Zwiebach at MIT in 2018: Quantum Physics III
  3. Extract of the course on QM at the University of Cambridge: Time dependent Perturbation Theory
  4. Perfect for a first contact with Quantum Mechanics at an undergraduate level: Introduction to Quantum Mechanics, Griffiths
  5. Notes of Noah Miller (Harvard) on the use of representation theory in QM, very well written (PDF): Representation Theory and Quantum Mechanics
  6. Very interesting book on the use of symmetries in QM by Zwicky (PDF): Symmetries of Quantum Mechanics
  7. Good book on the use of group theory in QM: Quantum Theory, Groups and Representations, Woit
  8. Representation theory in QM (PDF): Understanding Quantum Mechanical Systems with Spherical Symmetry via Representations of Lie Groups, Greif
  9. Lecture on Quantum Groups at Stanford
  10. Woit's lecture on Quantum Groups in 2009
  11. Dozens of problems in Quantum Mechanics, with solutions: Problems and Solutions in Quantum Mechanics, Tamvakis

Solid-State Physics

  1. A reference, well-written: Introduction to Solid state Physics, Kittel
  2. Probably the one that is the most widely used: Solid-State-Physics, Ashcroft & Mermin
  3. Collaborative solutions to Ashcroft and Mermin: dileepma
  4. Lots of exercises and course summary: Understanding solid state physics - problems and solutions, Cazaux
  5. Another batch of exercises to prepare for the exam: Solid State Physics: Problems and Solutions, Laszlo & Martin
  6. Lots of exercises, some of them in solid state physics: Problems and Solutions on Solid State Physics, Relativity and Miscellaneous Topics, Yung-Kuo

Statistical Physics

  1. I found the first chapters quite clear: Statistical Physics (2 ed), Huang
  2. Lecture notes at ETH Zürich by Prof. Sigrist: Statistical Physics 2014
  3. Lecture notes at the University of Cambridge by Prof. Tong: Statistical Physics

Particle and Nuclear Physics

  1. A very good introductory book on the subject: The Basis of Nuclear and Particle Physics, by Alexander Belyaev and Douglas Ross.
  2. A great summary of the experimental concepts in Nuclear and Particle Physics: Techniques for Nuclear and Particle Physics Experiments, W.R. Leo.
  3. Another good textbook on the subject: Particle Physics (3rd. ed.), by B.R. Martin and G. Shaw.
  4. Lots of solved problems to practice before the exam: Problems and Solutions of Atomic, Nuclear and Particle Physics, by Yung-Kuo Lim.
  5. There is also a book from D.J. Griffiths on the subject, 'Introduction to Elementary Particle Physics', but I did not find it as good as the author's other works.
  6. Finally, to go (way) beyond the scope of an undergraduate level course: Gauge Theory of Elementary Particle Physics, by Ta-Pei Cheng and Ling-Fong Li.

Quantum Field Theory

  1. YouTube playlist by Scientia Egregia (in French): Théorie quantique des champs
  2. Very well written: A very short introduction to quantum field theory, Stetz
  3. I can't but recommend Prof. David Tong's lecture notes on the subject, cf. his website at the top of this page.
  4. Good textbook on the subject: QFT and the Standard Model, Schwartz
  5. Easy to read: QFT in a nutshell, Zee
  6. Recording of Etingof's lecture at MIT: Geometry and QFT

String Theory

  1. Two introduction volumes by Polchinsky: String theory vol I & II
  2. Very good introductory book: A first course in string theory, Zwiebach
  3. Lectiure notes of Prof. Tong at the University of Cambridge: String Theory Tong
  4. Recording of Zwiebach's lectures at MIT: String Theory for Undergraduates
  5. Lui's lecture on String Theory and Holographic Duality at MIT
  6. A good website providing a motivation for String Theory

Gauge gravity/duality, supersymmetries

  1. Page of the lecture of the University of Cambridge, 2019: Aaron Wall
  2. A famous one: Gauge Theory of Elementary Particle Physics, by Ta-Pei Cheng and Ling-Fong Li.
  3. Lecture on spin chains and supersymmetric gauge theories at ETHZ (PDF): Reffert

Symmetries in Physics

  1. Lectures notes of Niklas Beisert, ETH Zürich, FS15: Symmetries in Physics

Interesting Talks

  1. Prof. Brian Swingle, University of Maryland: Black holes as extreme quantum information processors
  2. Prof. Juan Maldacena, Princeton: Black hole entropy and quantum information (ETHZ Pauli lectures 2022)
  3. Prof. David Tong, Cambridge: Quantum Fields: the true building blocks of the Universe
  4. Jim Al-Khalili: Quantum Life: how physics can revolutionise biology
