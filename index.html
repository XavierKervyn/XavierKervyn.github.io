Welcome on my personal website! I'm Xavier, currently enrolled in the Part III of the Mathematical Tripos (MASt in Applied Mathematics) at the University of Cambridge. I steer my cursus towards research at the crossroads of high energy gravitation theories. More specifically, I'd like to bring contributions to the following topics, to name but a few: Celestial Amplitudes and Flat Space Holography, Asymptotic Symmetries, Soft Theorems and Celestial CFT, Black Holes and the Information Paradox, Quantum Information, AdS/CFT and Black Hole Microstates, Gravitational Aspects and Twistor methods in String Theory...

This website serves two purposes. First, it allows me to showcase some of my recent academic activities. Second, it is a convenient tool for me to share various resources (lecture notes, textbooks, research opportunities, etc.) that I've found to be worth looking at in my journey so far. Check these out if you think that we share common interests!

Besides science, I am a fervent adept of outdoor and endurance sports in my free time. When not in Cambridge (aka the flattest place on Earth), you'll likely find me on an adventure in the mountains!

CERN - CMS ECAL detector

(2022 - Particle Physics) I had the chance to join the High-Energy Physics Group of ETH Zürich (CMS collaboration, CERN) to carry out a project under the supervision of Dr. Simone Pigazzini. Together with Noah Roux, I studied the properties of the early prototype of the upgraded electromagnetic calorimeter (ECAL) of the Compact Muon Solenoid (CMS) experiment with a class IV laser. In parallel, we also wrote a Python package to automate the analysis of events. This work is to be incorporated in the future CMS experiment the in the upcoming years in the frame the High-Luminosity phase of the LHC.

Large Interferometer For Exoplanets

(2021 - Astrophysics) The LIFE space mission aims at detecting and characterizing the atmospheres of hundreds of nearby extrasolar planets by means of a mid-infrared nulling interferometer concept. Under the supervision of Prof. Dr. Sascha P. Quanz and Felix Dannert, I implemented the phenomenon of imperfect nulling into the mission simulation software, and subsequently quantified and measured its impact on the detection yield.
