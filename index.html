Welcome!

I'm Xavier, currently enrolled in the Part III of the Mathematical Tripos (MASt in Applied Mathematics) at the University of Cambridge, UK. I steer my cursus towards the study of quantum gravity, i.e. research at the crossroads of high energy and gravitation theories.

On this website, I share some of my recent academic activities, as well as various resources (lectures, MOOCs, etc.) which I've found to be worth looking at in my journey so far.
Check these out if you think that we share common interests!

Resources

Unless stated otherwise, all ressources are available in English.

  1. Mathematics

    • Transition from high-school to university
      1. Webpage with lots of ressources to prepare for the French preparatory classes (in French): Maths à Saint-Cyr
    • Analysis, differential calculus
      1. Hundreds of exercises, aimed at undegraduate level (in French): Analyse – Recueil d’exercices et aide-mémoire, vol. I and vol. II, Jacques Douchet.
      2. The associated textbook (in French): Calcul Différentiel et Intégral, Jacques Douchet.
      3. A great YouTube playlist by 3 Blue 1 Brown, providing a great visualisation of the concepts (for undergraduates): Essence of Calculus
    • Linear algebra
      1. The GOAT, Grant Sanderson, a.k.a. 3 Blue 1 Brown (YouTube): Essence of linear algebra. Wonderful animations, really helpful to build an intuition of what's going on.
    • Differential equations
      1. A wonderful Youtube playlist by Michael Penn on the subject: Differential Equations
      2. Still on YouTube, by Mu Prime Maths this time: Dissecting Differential Equations
    • Representation Theory, Group Theory
      1. Easy to read, conveys a good intuition behind the concepts: Group theory in a Nutshell for physicists, Zee
      2. For a rigorous approach: Lie Groups, Lie Algebras and Representations, Brian Hall
      3. A very good script on Lie algebras and their representations (PDF): Runkel
      4. This one turned out to be a very good companion to the course "Symmetries, Particles and Fields" I attended in Cambridge: Symmetries, Lie Algebras and Representations, Fuchs & Schweigert.
    • Tensors and Differential Geometry
      1. YouTube playlists, by Eigenchris: Tensors for Beginners and Tensors Calculus
      2. A great textbook, 'Tensors for Physics' by Siegfried Hess.
      3. If you're a physicist like me, then you'd probably like to read the few chapters of Sean's Caroll book on Spacetime and Relativity, mentioned in the resources for Physics.
      4. Infos and lectures on Differential Geometry I at ETH Zürich: Course info and Lecture Notes (Prof. Urs Lang)

  2. Physics

    • General websites
      1. Archives of the Institute for Theoretical Physics at ETH Zürich: ITP Lecture Archive.
      2. Archives of the lectures at MIT: MIT OpenCourseware.
      3. Teaching page of Prof. Tong at the University of Cambridge.
      4. Lectures notes + exercises of the Part III in Applied Mathematics at the University of Cambridge
      5. Example sheets of the Mathematical Tripos (undergraduate curriculum) of the University of Cambridge.
      6. Online courses and recorded seminars at the Perimeter Institute for Theoretical Physics.
      7. Excellent lectures on the Geometrical anatomy of Theoretical Physics by Prof. Schüller
    • Classical Electrodynamics
      1. The perfect book for an introduction to Electrodynamics at an undergraduate level: Introduction to electrodynamics (4. ed), Griffiths
      2. Famous but quite old, and the problems are notoriously difficult: Classical Electrodynamics, Jackson. Collaborative solutions to the problems can be found here.
      3. My personal favourite, very complete and with lots of exercises: Modern Electrodynamics, Zangwill
    • Quantum Mechanics
      1. On the path integral approach to QM, by the great Feynman: Quantum Mechanics and Path Integrals, Feynman. For a first contact with QM, I would rather recommend the Hamiltonian approach (eg. Griffiths).
      2. Perfect for a first contact with Quantum Mechanics at an undergraduate level: Introduction to Quantum Mechanics, Griffiths
      3. Notes of Noah Miller (Harvard) on the use of representation theory in QM, very well written (PDF): Representation Theory and Quantum Mechanics
      4. Very interesting book on the use of symmetries in QM by Zwicky (PDF): Symmetries of Quantum Mechanics
      5. Representation theory in QM (PDF): Understanding Quantum Mechanical Systems with Spherical Symmetry via Representations of Lie Groups, Greif
      6. To practive before the exam: Problems and Solutions in Quantum Mechanics, Tamvakis
    • Solid-State Physics (certainly not my favourite topic, but some useful resources nonetheless)
      1. A reference, well-written: Introduction to Solid state Physics, Kittel
      2. Probably the one that is the most widely used: Solid-State-Physics, Ashcroft & Mermin. Collaborative solutions can be found here.
      3. Lots of exercises and an insightful course summary: Understanding solid state physics - problems and solutions, Cazaux
      4. Prepare for the exam: Solid State Physics: Problems and Solutions, Laszlo & Martin
      5. Lots of exercises, some of them in solid state physics: Problems and Solutions on Solid State Physics, Relativity and Miscellaneous Topics, Yung-Kuo
    • Statistical Physics
      1. I found the first chapters quite clear: Statistical Physics (2 ed), Huang
      2. Lecture notes at ETH Zürich by Prof. Sigrist: Statistical Physics 2014
      3. Checkout Prof. Tong's notes: Statistical Physics
    • Particle and Nuclear Physics
      1. A very good introductory book on the subject: The Basis of Nuclear and Particle Physics, by Alexander Belyaev and Douglas Ross.
      2. A great summary of the experimental concepts in Nuclear and Particle Physics: Techniques for Nuclear and Particle Physics Experiments, W.R. Leo.
      3. Another good textbook on the subject: Particle Physics (3rd. ed.), by B.R. Martin and G. Shaw.
      4. Lots of solved problems to practice before the exam: Problems and Solutions of Atomic, Nuclear and Particle Physics, by Yung-Kuo Lim.
      5. There is also a book from D.J. Griffiths on the subject, 'Introduction to Elementary Particle Physics', but I did not find it as good as the author's other works.
      6. Finally, to go (way) beyond the scope of an undergraduate level course: Gauge Theory of Elementary Particle Physics, by Ta-Pei Cheng and Ling-Fong Li.
